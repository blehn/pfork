    Still Corners

    Still Corners: "Fireflies"

    Sub Pop

    By Larry Fitzmaurice; September 28, 2012

    Back in the summer of 2010, London's Still Corners caught a few ears with their single "Endless Summer", a spooky slice of atmosphere that proved, amidst that year's beach-fixated blog mindset, that you could still get away with using the word "summer" in a song title if you had the goods to back it up. A year later, they signed to Sub Pop and released their debut LP, Creatures of an Hour, a scattershot album that mined a strain of distant, narcotized cool previously embraced by acts like Deerhunter and Broadcast (of which the latter's influence on modern indie, seemingly, looms larger with every passing year).

    "Fireflies", the A-side to Still Corners' new single in anticipation of their second album (out in the spring of 2013), is no less beholden to the past-- only, this time, the band's drawing from the spangly, huge-sounding sounds of 1980s synth-pop. The change suits them well, as it's their most immediate and starry-eyed song to date, with Tessa Murray's multi-tracked vocals navigating their way through gorgeously empty terrain dotted with cymbal washes and gently pulsing synth tones. 

    Still Corners: "Fireflies"

    [from the "Fireflies" b/w "Hearts of Fools" single; out this fall on Sub Pop]

    Andy Stott

    Andy Stott: "Numb"

    Modern Love

    By Larry Fitzmaurice; September 25, 2012

    Last year, Manchester-based producer Andy Stott rose to prominence with a double-dose of coruscated, dub techno, the Passed Me By and We Stay Together EPs. On October 29, he returns with a new album, Luxury Problems, on Modern Love. The record features vocal contributions from Alison Skidmore, Stott's former piano teacher; on opening album cut "Numb", her presence is felt immediately. The bulk of the track consists of her softly elided voice, folding in on itself to create a gently smothering cloud over a chugging, industrial techno beat. The last few years of electronic music have seen producers experimenting with new ways to process and edit the human voice; on "Numb", Stott offers his own inspired approach, retaining his haunted aesthetic while hinting at a thrilling new direction.

    Susanne Sundfør

    Susanne Sundfør: "White Foxes"

    EMI

    By Katherine St. Asaph; September 25, 2012

    Front page photo by Sofia Fredricks Sprung

    One of 2010's most overlooked albums-- outside Norway, at least-- was songwriter and pianist Susanne Sundfør's The Brothel.     Bat For Lashes

    Bat For Lashes: "Marilyn"

    Parlophone / Capitol / EMI

    By Mark Richardson; September 13, 2012

    The "Marilyn" in the title in the new song from the upcoming Bat for Lashes album,  The Haunted Man, is the one you're thinking of. The Marilyn, the one who lived like a candle in the wind and got Nicki Minaj thinking about insecurity. For Bat for Lashes' Natasha Khan, Marilyn stands in for feeling special. "Holding you, I'm touching a star," she sings on the chorus, "Turning into a Marilyn, leaning out of your big car." It sounds a bit like a Lana Del Rey lyric, mapping a rush of emotions onto media icons, which can make for a clever song but isn't for me especially relatable, since I've never for even the briefest moment felt like James Dean (or even Arthur Miller). But as usual with Bat for Lashes, the power of the song comes from the totality of thing, the way the sound and voice and lyrics fuse together into one breathless whole infused with drama.

    As widescreen electronic pop goes, the song evokes the over-the-top glitter of Giorgio Moroder's Top Gun-era production work from the mid-1980s, all crashing drums and sunburst synths and watching every motion in a foolish lover's game. Sonically, you feel small standing next to it, and want to let it carry you up the clouds. And then, at around the three-minute mark, comes one of my favorite musical moments of the year, as Khan's voice becomes a disembodied thing that haunts the rest of the song. "Put your star in me," she sings at one point. At its best moments (especially that gorgeous bridge) "Marilyn" is like the star collapsing in on itself, shooting sparks and dark energy in every direction.

    Bat for Lashes: "Marilyn" (via YouTube)

    [from The Haunted Man; out 10/15/12 in the UK via Parlophone/EMI]

    By most standards, Jay-Z and Kanye West are having a good year. G.O.O.D. Somehow, he makes it all seem relatable and humane, even though he's calling himself a vessel for God's words by the end. Is "Clique" center of the universe-type shit?     Angel Olsen

    Angel Olsen: "Acrobat"

    Bathetic

    By Laura Snapes; August 31, 2012

    It doesn't feel right that Angel Olsen's second album, Half Way Home, is only coming out next week. The two songs we've heard from it so far, "Tiniest Seed" and this, "Acrobat", feel like old gems from a larger, dustier treasure chest, a long lost record something akin to Linda Perhacs' Parallelograms, or Vashti Bunyan's Just Another Diamond Day. Fortunately for us, Olsen is firmly of the present, even though her songs intimate that there's only a gossamer film between her here and now, and passing beyond.

    "I thought… that I'd died," she sings, lingering on the vowels like a steady finger circling the lip of a glass of wine. Despite her crystalline voice, there's no need for the dreaded e-word that plagues many female singer/songwriters here; the thing that bolts Olsen firmly to earth is the guttural burr that sometimes jars her throat, turning her into a tragic cabaret singer for just a moment. Strangely, that's the voice she adopts to sing of life-- "I want to be made out of love/ I want to be made into life"-- in a song that starts off detailing how she wants to be "a bit like" someone, and act as their comforter and more. Without wishing to draw glib comparisons, Olsen's lyrical relationship with death is proving as profound as that of her old touring partner, Will Oldham; though Olsen's meditations on the subject don't seem to fear it as a final judgement, but invite it as a last state of transformation.

    The xx

    The xx: "Chained"

    Young Turks

    By Hari Ashurst; August 7, 2012

    The best xx songs are defined by their use of space. One of the most interesting uses of open air on the band's self-titled debut was the gulf between Romy Madley-Croft and Oliver Sim's voices.     Grizzly Bear: "Yet Again"

    "No surprise, this is often how it's done," Ed Droste confesses at the start of "Yet Again". Well, yes and no.     Tame Impala

    Tame Impala: "Elephant"

    Modular

    By Ian Cohen; July 25, 2012

    Tame Impala have never shied away from the sounds of classic rock radio, but "Elephant" is the first time they've gone deep into its mythology and symbolism. The first official single from their sophomore LP, Lonerism, initially sounds like a pure top-down songwriting exercise-- as in, "let's try to sound like the song's badass title." So, thick, one-note guitar riffs shuffle and stomp with a heavy beat, while Kevin Parker manages to rhyme "elephant" with "shakin' its big red trunk for the hell of it". But Tame Impala songs are never content to simply plod at one speed, and the song's strange syncopations and rhythmic shifts become hints that this beast can pirouette and shake, too. Abetted by Parker and Dave Fridmann's delectable retro-futurist production, "Elephant" splits wide open during its midsection for an expansive and lyrical guitar jam that these guys are doing better than just about anyone else right now.

    Jacques Greene

    Jacques Greene: "Ready"

    3024

    By Mike Powell; July 24, 2012

    While Montreal producer Jacques Greene's previous releases have been characterized by clubby, two-step send-ups of contemporary R&B, "Ready"-- the title track from his new EP on Martyn's 3024 label-- provides something different: A backdrop of terse, garage rhythms overlaid with Burial-level atmospherics.     Bat For Lashes

    Bat For Lashes: "Laura"

    Parlophone / EMI

    By Laura Snapes; July 23, 2012

    When Bat for Lashes' Natasha Khan released 2009's Two Suns, its lead single "Daniel" was an ode to teenage first loves and naïveté, with all its hopefulness pinned onto Daniel LaRusso from The Karate Kid. On "Laura", the first song offered from Bat for Lashes' third LP, The Haunted Man, Khan offers her reassurances to a friend who's been left behind, clinging to a lifestyle of parties and night flirtations when everyone else has grown up and settled down. The reassurances Khan offers this friend aren't all desirable scenarios-- what grown woman wants her name "tattooed on every boy's skin"?-- but they fit the contradiction that the titular dame embodies: wanting to cling to a scene that can't last, trying to prolong the hot flash of lust into that eternal green light across the bay. When Khan near-pleads, "You're more than a superstar," it sounds like the kind of well-meaning but empty sentiment you might impart to a weeping friend-- but the subtlety is that "Laura", whoever she is, is worth much more than validation through nightlife notoriety.
